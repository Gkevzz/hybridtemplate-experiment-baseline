# HybridBonus — Editorial Design Language (E1)

Status: Finalized. Immutable except through future constitutional amendments.

Scope: Visual communication principles only. No components, no implementation, no tokens, no spacing, no typography, no colors, no motion, no icons, no illustrations.

Position: Sits directly below E0 — Editorial UX Principles and above E2 — Editorial Component Library. E1 translates the principles established by E0 into a universal visual language for editorial communication. It does not introduce new editorial principles; it expresses existing ones visually.

## Part 1 — Purpose of Editorial Design

Visual design at HybridBonus is not a layer of polish applied after the content is written. It is an editorial tool. Every visual decision exists to help the reader understand, compare, trust, orient, learn, or decide.

The purpose of editorial design is to make information easier to process than it would be in plain prose alone. This means:

- The right idea is found quickly.
- The relationship between ideas is visible at a glance.
- The importance of each idea matches its visual weight.
- The reader understands what kind of information they are looking at.
- The reader knows what is fact, what is analysis, and what is recommendation.
- The reader can validate what they read without hunting.
- The reader can compare alternatives without reconstructing them from memory.

Visuals are not decoration. They are not mood. They are not brand expression for its own sake. They are communication devices. If a visual does not improve understanding, reduce cognitive load, build trust, or support a decision, it does not belong.

Editorial design serves E0 by translating abstract principles into concrete visual behavior:

- Understanding before persuasion becomes a hierarchy where the answer is seen before the reasoning.
- Progressive disclosure becomes a visual rhythm that separates summary from detail.
- Transparency becomes signals that show verification, freshness, and source without asking.
- Calm over excitement becomes restraint in visual energy, color, and motion.
- Reader autonomy becomes decision support that is visible but not coercive.
- Consistency creates trust becomes a visual language that is learned once and recognized everywhere.

## Part 2 — Visual Communication Principles

The following principles govern every visual decision in HybridBonus editorial content.

1. Clarity before decoration. A beautiful element that obscures meaning is a failure. A plain element that clarifies meaning is a success. Visual refinement is welcome only when it does not compete with the information.

2. Recognition before novelty. Readers should not have to learn a new visual language on every page. Familiar forms, consistent treatments, and repeated patterns reduce effort and increase trust. Novelty is justified only when it communicates something that existing patterns cannot.

3. Consistency before creativity. Creative variation is a cost paid by the reader. Consistency is a gift. The same kind of information should look the same everywhere. Exceptions must be meaningful, not stylistic.

4. Explanation before attraction. A visual element that draws attention without explaining something is a distraction. Visual prominence is a tool for directing the reader to what matters, not for making the page more engaging.

5. Calm before excitement. The visual energy of the page must match the reader's task: understanding a complex, consequential choice. Celebration, urgency, and high-contrast drama are forbidden. The product should feel quiet, considered, and stable. The test: a visual is calm when removing it would not lower the reader's emotional temperature. If removal would calm the page further, the visual has failed.

6. Information before aesthetics. Aesthetic decisions are evaluated by their effect on comprehension. Color, shape, weight, and space are chosen to carry meaning, not to express a mood.

7. Function before flourish. Every flourish, texture, gradient, or decorative element must serve a function. If it can be removed without changing what the reader understands, it should be removed.

8. Structure before styling. The first design decision is how information is organized, not how it looks. Once the structure is correct, the visual treatment follows. Styling cannot rescue poor structure.

9. Restraint before richness. A page with fewer visual ideas is easier to read than a page with more. Richness is added only when the information genuinely demands it.

10. Honesty before persuasion. Visual emphasis must not be used to make a weaker option appear stronger, a risk appear smaller, or an action feel more urgent than it is. Visual language is bound by the same integrity as prose.

## Part 3 — Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy is the order in which the eye should travel. It must match the editorial order of importance.

Primary information is what the reader came to learn. It answers the question the page is about. It receives the highest visual prominence: position, scale, weight, and space all say "this is the point."

Supporting information is what the reader needs to understand the primary information. It receives secondary prominence: clearly visible, but subordinate.

Contextual information is what helps the reader orient themselves or place the current idea in a broader picture. It receives tertiary prominence: present, quiet, and available on demand.

Metadata is the administrative context of the information: source, date, verification status, author. It must be visible at the right moment but must never dominate the idea it supports. Metadata belongs near the claim it verifies.

What must never receive visual emphasis:

- Decorative elements.
- Affiliate calls to action above the information they depend on.
- Secondary or tertiary information before primary information.
- Marketing language at the same level as editorial claims.
- Unverified claims at the same level as verified facts.
- Caveats or limitations hidden where the eye does not naturally travel.

Where an independence signal and a commercial element compete for the same visual region, the independence signal wins.

The test of visual hierarchy is simple: if a reader were to see only the most prominent elements, they should still understand the core message of the page. If they cannot, the hierarchy is wrong.

## Part 4 — Visual Semantics

Visual treatment communicates meaning. Every recurring visual choice becomes a word in the reader's vocabulary. Shape, weight, color role, position, and grouping are not aesthetic choices; they are semantic choices.

Similar information should always look similar. Two facts of the same kind — two verification dates, two bonus conditions, two trust signals — should share the same visual treatment. The reader learns the pattern once and applies it everywhere.

Different information should intentionally look different. When two pieces of information differ in kind, their visual treatment should reinforce that difference. A warning is not a fact. A recommendation is not an analysis. A pending state is not a verified state. The reader should be able to tell them apart before reading the words.

Visual distinction exists only to reinforce editorial distinction. If two things are different in appearance but not in meaning, the visual difference is noise. If two things are different in meaning but visually identical, the visual language has failed.

Visual similarity creates recognition; recognition creates trust. Readers should learn the language once and understand it everywhere. A verification signal that changes shape from page to page is not a signal — it is a puzzle. A consistent visual language builds trust because it is predictable.

This principle becomes the philosophical parent of future visual building blocks:

- A badge exists because a categorical signal needs to be recognized at a glance.
- A warning exists because some information changes how the reader should read what surrounds it.
- An info box exists because a definition or caveat needs to be visually separated from the main flow.
- A comparison exists because alternatives need to be read against each other.
- A timeline exists because sequence or duration is the point.
- A trust indicator exists because verification, freshness, or independence must be visibly understood.

None of these are components yet. They are visual semantic needs: recurring categories of meaning that the language must be able to express.

## Part 5 — Information Visualization

Information becomes visual when the visual form reveals meaning faster, more accurately, or more completely than prose alone.

A piece of information should become structured when it has a recurring internal shape — for example, a bonus that always contains an amount, conditions, wagering, and expiration. Structure makes the pattern visible.

A piece of information should become visual when its meaning is relational or spatial — for example, how one operator compares to another across several dimensions, or how a sequence of steps unfolds. The eye processes relationships more efficiently than the memory reconstructs them.

A piece of information should become comparative when the reader's question is "which one?" or "how do they differ?" Comparison requires alignment: the same dimensions shown for each option, in the same order, so the reader can scan down or across.

A piece of information should become sequential when order matters — for example, the steps to activate a bonus, the timeline of a withdrawal, or the lifecycle of a verification. Sequence must be read as sequence, not as a list that happens to be ordered.

A piece of information should become grouped when several distinct facts belong to one larger idea. Grouping makes the idea hold together visually so the reader perceives it as a unit.

A piece of information should become summarized when the reader needs the gist before the detail. A summary is not a replacement for detail; it is an invitation to it.

A piece of information should become a warning when ignoring it could lead to harm or misunderstanding. Warnings are not decorative highlights; they are structural redirections.

The decision to visualize is always editorial, not stylistic. A chart that explains less than a sentence is not a visualization — it is a decoration. A table with two rows is often a sentence. A diagram with one path is often a list.

## Part 6 — Trust Visualization

Trust is a visual quality at HybridBonus. The reader must be able to see, at a glance, what they can rely on and what they cannot.

Verified information must be visually distinct from unverified information. The reader should not need to read carefully to know that a fact has been checked.

Pending information must be visible as pending, not hidden as missing. A placeholder state is a first-class visual state.

Outdated information must be visible as outdated. Freshness is part of the fact; its visual state must communicate that.

Editorial analysis must be visually distinct from factual information. The reader must always know when they are reading HybridBonus's reasoning rather than a verified fact.

Recommendations must be visually distinct from analysis and fact. A recommendation is a conclusion; it must not wear the same visual clothes as the evidence it rests on.

The three visual states — fact, analysis, recommendation — correspond permanently to E0's Layer 1, Layer 2, and Layer 3. The mapping is fixed and may not be redefined by E2 or E3.

Uncertainty must be visible. "We do not yet know" is a complete and honest visual state, not an absence of design.

Sources must be visible near the claims they support. The path from claim to source must be short and obvious.

Editorial independence must be communicated visually where commercial relationships appear. Independence is not a footer topic; it is a visible property of the page.

Responsible gambling must be visually present and never demoted. The responsible path is always visible.

Where HybridBonus expresses per-category editorial judgment as a structured trust profile, that profile is the canonical visualization of editorial assessment. Its categories are governed by E0; E1 governs only how those categories are visually expressed. No total score, no composite ranking, and no algorithm visualization, ever — regardless of what the profile is later named or how it evolves.

The visual language of trust is not a set of icons. It is a consistent relationship between what is shown and what it means. The reader should learn the grammar once and read it everywhere.

## Part 7 — Educational Visualization

Some ideas are easier to learn visually than textually. Educational visualization is the use of visual form to build mental models.

A concept should be visualized when:

- It is relational — the reader needs to see how parts connect.
- It is procedural — the reader needs to follow steps in order.
- It is comparative — the reader needs to see differences side by side.
- It is abstract — the reader needs a concrete anchor to understand it.
- It is dimensional — the reader needs to weigh multiple factors at once.

The goal is not to replace explanation but to accelerate it. A good educational visual is one that the reader can read in a glance and then understand the prose more deeply.

Examples of visual teaching forms:

- A flow that shows how a bonus moves from claim to wagering to withdrawal.
- A comparison that shows how two wagering requirements produce different real-world costs.
- A diagram that shows where a verification date sits in the information lifecycle.
- A checklist that shows the conditions a player must satisfy before withdrawing.

Visuals should always be paired with textual explanation. A visual without a caption is ambiguous; a caption without a visual is often slower. Together they teach faster than either alone.

Educational visualization must respect the reader's prior knowledge. The same concept can be shown simply on a review page and in more depth on a guide page. The visual language must scale from one-line to deep without inventing new grammar.

## Part 8 — Reading Rhythm

A page should naturally alternate between reading and seeing. The reader should never face a long wall of uninterrupted text, nor a long sequence of visuals without prose to anchor them.

The ideal rhythm is:

- prose
- visual
- prose
- structured module
- prose
- callout
- diagram
- summary

This alternation does not mean every paragraph must be followed by an image. It means that the page should be structured as a conversation between the reader's eye and the reader's understanding. Long prose sections break into smaller units. Dense visual sections are introduced and concluded in prose.

The rhythm serves several functions:

- It prevents fatigue.
- It signals progression.
- It gives the reader natural stopping points.
- It makes the page feel scannable and the depth feel optional.
- It turns a monologue into a dialogue.

The rhythm is also mobile-first. On a small screen, the alternation is even more important because the reader's field of view is smaller and interruptions are more frequent. A page that works on mobile has already been disciplined for rhythm.

A section should be re-enterable. The reader should be able to return after a distraction and find their place because the structure is regular and the visual anchors are clear.

No prose section should exceed roughly three mobile screens without a visual anchor. No visual sequence should run without a prose anchor introducing and concluding it.

## Part 9 — Information Density

Density is not the enemy. Clutter is.

Rich is when many pieces of information are present and each is legible. The reader can scan, compare, and return without feeling overwhelmed.

Cluttered is when too many competing elements fight for attention. The reader does not know where to look. Visual density exceeds cognitive capacity.

Dense is when information is packed efficiently. The reader can hold more in working memory because structure does the organizing work.

Difficult is when the reader must work to extract meaning. Dense information is not difficult if it is well-structured.

Minimal is when every element earns its place. The page is quiet because it is disciplined, not because it is empty.

Empty is when information that belongs on the page is missing. Minimalism is not an excuse to omit necessary context.

Additional visual structure improves comprehension when it:

- Groups related information.
- Separates different kinds of information.
- Reveals relationships that prose would hide.
- Repeats a pattern the reader can learn.
- Reduces the need to hold information in memory.

Additional visual structure harms comprehension when it:

- Introduces variety without meaning.
- Competes with the primary information.
- Makes a simple idea look complex.
- Adds visual maintenance without editorial benefit.

The test is always: does the structure help the reader process more, or does it make the reader work harder?

Default state for editorial pages is Dense. Rich is permitted for comparisons. Minimal is permitted for educational intros and summary surfaces. Cluttered, Difficult, and Empty are always failures.

## Part 10 — Editorial Communication Patterns

HybridBonus content follows recurring communication patterns. Each pattern is a visual and editorial arc.

Pattern 1 — Question → Answer → Reasoning → Evidence → Recommendation
- The reader's question is stated or implied.
- The answer is given immediately.
- The reasoning explains why this answer is the right one.
- Evidence is shown: sources, dates, facts, comparisons.
- A recommendation follows, but only after the reader has everything needed to evaluate it.

Pattern 2 — Problem → Explanation → Example → Decision Support
- A problem or risk is named.
- The explanation makes the mechanism clear.
- A concrete example grounds it in reality.
- Decision support helps the reader apply it to their own situation.

Pattern 3 — Concept → Definition → Application → Caveat
- A concept is introduced.
- It is defined in plain terms.
- Its application is shown.
- Caveats are stated before the reader overgeneralizes.

Pattern 4 — Comparison → Dimensions → Trade-offs → Fit
- Two or more options are introduced.
- They are compared across the same dimensions.
- Trade-offs are made explicit.
- Fit is determined by the reader's profile, not a universal verdict.

Pattern 5 — Warning → Why It Matters → What to Do
- A warning is presented.
- Its significance is explained.
- The reader is told what to check, avoid, or do.

Pattern 6 — Summary → Detail → Sources → Related Reading
- The reader gets the summary first.
- Detail is available on demand.
- Sources are visible.
- Related reading extends the topic without leaving the reader stranded.

These patterns are not page templates. They are communication structures. A single page may contain several of them. A complex page may nest them. The visual language must be able to express each pattern clearly and consistently.

## Part 11 — Cross-Page Recognition

A reader who arrives on any HybridBonus page should immediately know they are still inside HybridBonus. Recognition is a trust mechanism.

Cross-page recognition is built on:

- A consistent information hierarchy (question → answer → reasoning → evidence → deep dive).
- A consistent visual semantic vocabulary (what a warning looks like, what a verified fact looks like, what a recommendation looks like).
- A consistent reading rhythm (alternation between prose and structured information).
- A consistent tone and density (calm, structured, honest).
- A consistent placement of trust signals (verification, freshness, sources, independence).
- A consistent treatment of commercial elements (affiliate disclosures, CTAs, commercial context).

This applies across all content types:

- Reviews
- Guides
- Comparisons
- Educational content
- Future content types not yet defined

A reader moving from a review to a comparison should not need to relearn how to read HybridBonus. The page type changes; the language does not.

Cross-page recognition does not mean visual uniformity. A comparison page and a guide page will look different because they do different work. But the differences must be systematic and meaningful, not arbitrary.

## Part 12 — Visual Trust Signals

Trust signals are visual expressions of the editorial properties the reader needs to verify. They must be obvious and honest.

Verification. A verified claim must look verified. An unverified claim must look unverified. A pending claim must look pending. These states must be visually distinct and never confused.

Freshness. A date is not a footnote; it is a visual property of the fact. Fresh information and aged information should be distinguishable at a glance.

Sources. The source path must be visible. The reader should see that a claim is sourced before they decide to inspect the source.

Editorial independence. Where commercial relationships appear, independence must be visually communicated. The reader should not have to search for disclosure.

Responsible gambling. Help, limits, and self-exclusion tools must be visually present. They must never be hidden, minimized, or styled as secondary.

Uncertainty. When HybridBonus does not know something, that absence must be visible as a state, not as a blank space.

Corrections. When information changes, the change must be visible. Silent updates are forbidden.

Trust states must be expressed through at least two visual channels — shape, position, weight, label, or color. Icon alone, color alone, and weight alone are insufficient.

The visual language of trust must be consistent enough that a single glance tells the reader the status of a claim. If the reader must read carefully to know whether something is verified, the trust signal has failed.

## Part 13 — Visual Consistency

Readers should never have to relearn the interface. Consistency is not a style choice; it is a comprehension and trust strategy.

Consistency operates at three levels:

Level 1 — Semantic consistency. The same kind of information always means the same thing. A warning always warns. A verification signal always verifies. A recommendation always recommends.

Level 2 — Structural consistency. The same kind of information always appears in the same kind of structure. A bonus is always presented through the same information fields. A comparison always aligns the same dimensions.

Level 3 — Visual consistency. The same kind of information always looks similar. The visual treatment reinforces the semantic and structural consistency.

A violation at any level creates confusion. A warning that looks like a tip is a semantic violation. A comparison that uses different dimensions for each option is a structural violation. A verification signal that changes shape is a visual violation.

Consistency also creates efficiency. A reader who learns the language on one page reads faster on the next. An editor who knows the pattern produces better content. A future component that follows the language fits naturally into the existing system.

Consistency must not become rigidity. New patterns are allowed when they express a meaning that existing patterns cannot. But the default is always to express through the existing language.

## Part 14 — Scalability

Part 14 governs editorial volume: more content expressed through the same patterns.

The visual language must make the five-hundredth review easier to build than the fifth. This is editorial scalability, not technical scalability.

Scalability is achieved when the language contains enough general patterns that new content fits existing structures rather than requiring new inventions.

A scalable visual language:

- Defines patterns, not instances.
- Distinguishes types of information rather than styling every unique fact.
- Builds repetition into the system so that new pages reuse established visual grammar.
- Avoids one-off visual solutions for recurring problems.
- Makes the addition of a new operator, bonus, or feature a matter of filling a known structure, not designing a new one.

The goal is not to eliminate all custom work. The goal is to ensure that custom work is reserved for genuinely unique editorial needs, while routine editorial needs are served by routine patterns.

Scalability also protects consistency. When the five-hundredth review is built from the same language as the first, the reader experiences a single product, not a collection of pages designed by different people at different times.

## Part 15 — Editorial Visual Anti-Patterns

A permanent blacklist. These are not design preferences. They are violations of the editorial purpose.

- Never use decorative visuals that carry no informational value.
- Never use visual elements that draw attention without explaining anything.
- Never use visual hierarchy to make a secondary claim look primary.
- Never use visual hierarchy to make a marketing claim look editorial.
- Never use visual similarity to make different kinds of information look the same.
- Never use visual difference to make similar information look different.
- Never use charts that explain less than the prose they replace.
- Never use tables with so few rows that a sentence would be clearer.
- Never use diagrams with only one path or one step.
- Never use timelines when the sequence is obvious or irrelevant.
- Never use comparison structures when there is only one option.
- Never use warnings as decorative highlights.
- Never use badges to create visual variety without categorical meaning.
- Never use color to carry meaning alone.
- Never use motion to attract attention to commercial actions.
- Never use motion to create excitement, urgency, or celebration.
- Never use visual effects that mimic gambling mechanics — flashing, spinning, near-miss, jackpot energy.
- Never use visual cues that imply scarcity, countdown, or FOMO.
- Never use visual noise that competes with the primary information.
- Never overload a page with competing visual ideas.
- Never let a visual style override the editorial hierarchy.
- Never let a visual style make a warning feel like a feature.
- Never let a visual style make a recommendation feel like a fact.
- Never let a visual style make an unverified claim feel verified.
- Never use visual language that changes from page to page without editorial reason.
- Never optimize visual design for engagement metrics that conflict with comprehension or trust.
- Never introduce a new visual pattern when an existing pattern can express the same meaning.
- Never use visual complexity to signal expertise or sophistication.

Motion is permitted only to clarify state change, reveal structure, confirm a reader action, or improve accessibility. All decorative, promotional, urgency-driven, and emotionally manipulative motion is forbidden.

This list grows over time. It never shrinks.

## Part 16 — Future Compatibility

Part 16 governs editorial evolution: new kinds of content expressed through the same language.

E1 intentionally avoids defining individual visual solutions. It defines the language that solutions must speak.

Future modules, diagrams, interaction patterns, educational systems, comparison systems, AI-assisted features, and content types inherit this language rather than extending it. The language evolves by refinement, not replacement.

Future visual innovations must be expressed through the existing language whenever possible. New visual patterns are introduced only when an existing pattern cannot communicate the required meaning.

This protects the system from design drift. It also makes future work faster: the answer to "How should this look?" is usually "Like the existing language already says it should."

Future compatibility means:

- The language is defined at a level of abstraction that survives new content types.
- The principles are stable even when the components change.
- New patterns are added only when justified, and they are then integrated into the language, not kept as exceptions.
- The manifesto remains the north star across all future versions.

## Editorial Design Manifesto

The Manifesto is the canonical summary of E1. It restates the principles defined in the constitutional body — it does not replace, override, or extend them. Where wording appears to differ, the relevant Part governs.

The permanent visual counterpart to the E0 Editorial UX Manifesto.

1. We exist to help Swedish players understand gambling — and our visual language serves that purpose first.
2. Clarity is the product. Beauty never outranks clarity. Visual hierarchy must always reflect editorial hierarchy.
3. We design information, not decoration. Every visual element must earn its place by improving understanding, reducing cognitive load, or supporting a better decision.
4. Similar information looks similar. Different information looks different. Visual distinction exists only to reinforce editorial distinction.
5. We are calm. The industry is loud; we are not. Our visual language never creates excitement, urgency, or pressure.
6. We visualize trust. Verification, freshness, uncertainty, and independence are always visible.
7. We teach visually. When a picture explains faster than prose, we use it — but never without the prose that anchors it.
8. We design for rhythm. A page alternates between reading and seeing so the reader never faces a wall of text.
9. We design for recognition. A reader who learns the language on one page reads every page better.
10. We scale through patterns. The five-hundredth review should be easier to build than the fifth.
11. We never manipulate with visual emphasis. What is prominent must be what matters.
12. We future-proof the language. New patterns are born only when old patterns cannot speak.
13. If a visual choice does not improve understanding, reduce cognitive load, or support a better decision, it does not belong on HybridBonus.
